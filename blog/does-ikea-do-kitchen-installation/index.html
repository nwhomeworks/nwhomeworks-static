Does IKEA Do Kitchen Installation? What You Need to Know

March 24, 2026 • By Tim Hutchinson

It's one of the most common questions I get: does IKEA do kitchen installation? The short answer is yes — sort of. The longer answer, from someone who's installed hundreds of IKEA kitchens across Seattle, Bellevue, Tacoma, and the rest of the Puget Sound, is a lot more nuanced. Let me break it down.

Yes, IKEA Offers Installation — But Here's the Catch

IKEA partners with TaskRabbit to offer kitchen installation services. You can arrange it right through the IKEA website or in-store after you've finalized your kitchen plan. A TaskRabbit "Tasker" will come to your home, assemble the flat-pack cabinets, and mount them to your walls.

Sounds great, right? Here's what they don't tell you upfront: IKEA's installation service only covers cabinet assembly and mounting. That's it. No plumbing. No electrical. No countertops. No backsplash. No flooring. No appliance hookups. All of those — the things that actually make your kitchen functional — are on you to arrange separately.

So if you're imagining a turnkey experience where IKEA handles everything from flat-pack to finished kitchen, that's not what you're getting. You'll likely need to coordinate two, three, maybe four different contractors to get the full job done. And that's where things can get complicated — and expensive — fast.

How Much Does IKEA Kitchen Installation Cost?

IKEA's TaskRabbit installation typically runs $100–$250 per cabinet for assembly and mounting. For a typical kitchen with 15–20 cabinets, that's roughly $1,500–$5,000 just for the cabinets to go on the wall.

But remember — that doesn't include any of the finish work. Once you add in a plumber, electrician, countertop fabricator, tile installer, and flooring crew, you're looking at a significantly larger total. Here's a rough breakdown of what a complete IKEA kitchen installation actually costs:

  • Cabinet assembly & mounting: $1,500–$5,000
  • Plumbing (sink, dishwasher, disposal): $800–$2,500
  • Electrical (lighting, outlets, appliance circuits): $500–$2,000
  • Countertop fabrication & install: $2,000–$6,000+
  • Tile backsplash: $800–$2,500
  • Flooring: $1,000–$3,000

All in, a complete IKEA kitchen remodel in the Seattle area typically runs $15,000–$35,000+ depending on the size of the kitchen, your material choices, and how much structural or layout work is involved. The IKEA cabinets themselves are a bargain — it's everything else that adds up.

One thing I tell clients: the real value of IKEA isn't just the cabinet price. It's that you're getting European-style, full-access cabinet construction — the same style that custom cabinet shops charge $30,000–$60,000 for — at a fraction of the cost. The trick is getting them installed right.

Does IKEA Kitchen Installation Include Plumbing?

No. This is probably the biggest surprise for folks planning an IKEA kitchen. IKEA's installation through TaskRabbit does not include plumbing, electrical, or any trade work. The Taskers assemble and hang cabinets — period.

For a kitchen remodel, you almost always need plumbing work. Even if you're keeping the sink in the same spot, disconnecting and reconnecting supply lines, drain lines, dishwasher hookups, and garbage disposals is skilled work that needs to be done right. And if you're moving the sink to a new location (like that island you've been dreaming about), you're looking at a much bigger plumbing job that involves running new supply and drain lines — sometimes through the floor or concrete slab.

This is one of the big reasons we handle IKEA kitchen installations as a complete project. Our plumbers, electricians, and tile team work together with our cabinet installers, so nothing falls through the cracks and the whole project stays on schedule.

Can You Install an IKEA Kitchen Yourself?

Can you? Technically, yes. IKEA designs their cabinet system to be DIY-friendly, and the assembly instructions are better than most. If you're handy, patient, and have a free weekend (or three), you can absolutely assemble and install IKEA cabinets yourself.

But here's what I've learned after hundreds of installs: the assembly is the easy part. It's what comes next that trips people up.

  • Walls aren't straight. Especially in Seattle's older homes. Getting a run of cabinets perfectly level and plumb when your walls are a half-inch out of square takes experience and patience.
  • IKEA's rail system is unforgiving. The SEKTION cabinets hang on a metal rail that needs to be dead level. If the rail is off, every cabinet is off — and shimming your way back to plumb is tedious work.
  • Filler pieces and end panels need to be cut precisely. IKEA gives you some, but odd corners, out-of-square walls, and non-standard layouts mean custom cuts.
  • The finishing details — toe kicks, crown molding, light valances, end panels — are where a kitchen goes from "I did this myself" to "wait, this is IKEA?" And that's where experience really shows.

I've had plenty of clients who started the install themselves and called us to finish. No shame in that — it's genuinely harder than it looks. I've also seen DIY installs where the homeowner did a great job. It really depends on your skill level, your tools, and honestly, your tolerance for frustration.

How Hard Is It to Install an IKEA Kitchen?

On a scale of "hanging a shelf" to "rewiring your house," I'd put an IKEA kitchen installation at about a 7 out of 10. The individual steps aren't that complicated. But there are a lot of them, they all need to be done in the right order, and mistakes compound fast.

Here's what a typical IKEA kitchen installation actually involves:

  1. Demo — removing old cabinets, countertops, backsplash, and sometimes flooring
  2. Rough trade work — moving or adding plumbing, electrical, and gas lines to match the new layout
  3. Wall prep — patching, leveling, and sometimes reframing to support the new cabinets
  4. Rail installation — mounting the SEKTION suspension rail perfectly level
  5. Cabinet assembly — building each cabinet from the flat-pack (the fun part, honestly)
  6. Cabinet mounting & alignment — hanging, leveling, shimming, and connecting cabinets together
  7. Filler, trim, and end panels — custom-cutting pieces to fill gaps and finish exposed sides
  8. Countertop template & install — usually a separate fabricator for stone, or DIY for butcher block
  9. Backsplash — tile, stone, or other material behind the counters
  10. Finish plumbing & electrical — connecting the sink, dishwasher, disposal, outlets, and fixtures
  11. Appliance installation — hooking up and testing everything
  12. Final adjustments — door and drawer alignment, soft-close tuning, handles and pulls

When we do a full IKEA kitchen installation, the project typically takes 2–4 weeks from demo to done, depending on the size and complexity. A DIY install with no trade work might take a dedicated weekend for just the cabinets — but getting to a finished, functional kitchen takes significantly longer.

Does IKEA Install Kitchen Countertops?

IKEA sells countertops, but their installation service doesn't cover stone or quartz. They offer laminate and wood countertops that are designed for DIY installation, and some Taskers may install these. But if you're going with quartz, granite, or any stone countertop (which most of our clients choose), you'll need a separate fabricator.

Here's how countertops typically work with an IKEA kitchen:

  1. Cabinets are installed and leveled first
  2. A countertop fabricator comes to template (measure) the installed cabinets
  3. The countertop is fabricated off-site (usually 1–2 weeks)
  4. The fabricator returns to install the finished countertop
  5. Sink and faucet are connected after the countertop is in

We work with local fabricators here in the Puget Sound who do excellent work and have quick turnaround times. It's one of the advantages of working with a general contractor who handles the full project — we coordinate the countertop timeline so you're not waiting around with a kitchen you can't use.

Does IKEA Kitchen Price Include Installation?

No. The price you see for an IKEA kitchen plan — whether it's $3,000 or $12,000 — is for the cabinets, doors, drawer fronts, hinges, and hardware only. Installation is a separate cost, whether you go through IKEA's TaskRabbit service or hire an independent installer.

A good rule of thumb: budget roughly 2–3x the cost of the IKEA cabinets for the complete installation, including all trade work, countertops, and backsplash. So if your IKEA kitchen plan comes to $5,000 in cabinets, expect $10,000–$15,000 total to get to a finished kitchen. More if you're doing structural work, moving plumbing, or choosing premium materials.

Even at those numbers, an IKEA kitchen is a phenomenal value. You're getting the same style and functionality as a $40,000+ custom kitchen for a fraction of the cost.

Why Hire a Professional IKEA Kitchen Installer?

I'm biased, obviously — this is what I do. But here's what I've seen after installing IKEA kitchens across Seattle for over a decade:

The biggest advantage of hiring a pro isn't the cabinet installation itself — it's having one person coordinate the entire project. When you go through IKEA's TaskRabbit service, you get someone who hangs cabinets. Everything else — plumbing, electrical, countertops, tile, appliances — is your problem to schedule, coordinate, and troubleshoot.

When you work with a general contractor who specializes in IKEA installations, you get:

  • One point of contact for the entire project
  • Coordinated scheduling across all trades
  • Someone who's seen every quirk and problem IKEA cabinets can throw at you
  • Custom modifications and "IKEA hacks" that overcome the system's limitations
  • A finished kitchen that looks custom, not flat-pack

We've installed hundreds of IKEA kitchens, and at this point there isn't much we haven't seen. Cabinets that need to be trimmed to fit a wonky wall. Dishwasher panels that need custom mounting. Toe-kick drawers that IKEA doesn't make. Appliance garages built from modified wall cabinets. We've done all of it — and if it's something new, we're genuinely excited to figure it out.

The Bottom Line

IKEA makes an excellent kitchen cabinet system. It's well-designed, affordable, and when installed correctly, it looks and performs like cabinetry costing three or four times the price. But "installed correctly" is doing a lot of heavy lifting in that sentence.

Whether you go DIY, use IKEA's TaskRabbit service, or hire a professional installer, go in with realistic expectations about the scope of work. The cabinets are just one piece of the puzzle. The plumbing, electrical, countertops, backsplash, and finishing details are what turn a pile of flat-packs into a kitchen you'll love for years.
